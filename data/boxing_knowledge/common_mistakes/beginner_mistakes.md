# Top 10 Beginner Boxing Mistakes

Every beginner makes mistakes -- that is how learning works. The difference between a beginner who progresses quickly and one who plateaus is the ability to identify and correct these mistakes early. Here are the ten most common errors and how to fix them.

## 1. Dropping the Hands

**What It Looks Like**: After throwing a punch, the hand drifts down to chest or waist level instead of returning to the chin.

**Why It Happens**: Fatigue, lack of discipline, or focusing so much on the punch that the return is forgotten.

**Why It Matters**: A dropped guard is an open invitation for a counter punch. In sparring, this gets you hit. On the BoxBunny, the CV system tracks guard position and the AI coach will flag this.

**How to Fix**:
- After every single punch, consciously return the fist to the cheekbone.
- Drill "return to guard" as a separate movement. Punch, return, check. Punch, return, check.
- Place a tennis ball between your glove and cheek. If it falls, your hand dropped.
- Start slow and make the return automatic before adding speed.

## 2. Closing the Eyes

**What It Looks Like**: Flinching and shutting the eyes when a punch comes toward the face, or closing them during a punch thrown toward the bag.

**Why It Happens**: Natural flinch reflex. The brain perceives incoming danger and triggers the eye-close response.

**Why It Matters**: You cannot counter, block, or slip what you cannot see. Closing the eyes makes you a stationary target.

**How to Fix**:
- Have a partner throw light, slow punches toward your face (not making contact). Practice keeping eyes open.
- Work the double-end bag, which snaps back at your face. Teaches you to stay visual.
- Wear a face cage during drills to build confidence that your guard will protect you.
- Gradually increase the speed and intensity of partner drills.

## 3. No Hip Rotation

**What It Looks Like**: Punching with the arm only. The hips stay square and static.

**Why It Happens**: Beginners focus on the hand and forget that power comes from the ground up through the kinetic chain.

**Why It Matters**: Without hip rotation, punches have no power, no reach, and no snap. Arm-only punches are slow and weak.

**How to Fix**:
- Practice hip rotation without punching. Stand in stance and rotate the hips left and right 50 times.
- Then add the hand. The hip moves first, the hand follows.
- Film yourself from the side. If your hips are not visibly rotating, you are arm-punching.
- Heavy bag work with focus on feeling the bag move from hip power, not arm push.

## 4. Leaning Forward

**What It Looks Like**: The upper body tilts forward when punching, particularly on the cross and jab. The head moves past the lead knee.

**Why It Happens**: Trying to reach an opponent or target that is slightly out of range.

**Why It Matters**: Leaning forward puts you off balance, makes it easy to push you down, and removes your ability to retreat quickly if needed.

**How to Fix**:
- If the target is out of range, step forward with the jab instead of leaning.
- Drill against a wall: stand one arm's length from the wall and throw punches. If your head touches the wall, you are leaning.
- Think "punch through, not forward." The punch travels horizontally; your weight stays centred over your base.

## 5. Flat Feet

**What It Looks Like**: The entire foot is on the ground, heels included. The fighter looks planted and heavy.

**Why It Happens**: Beginners are not yet conditioned for the calf endurance required to stay on the balls of the feet.

**Why It Matters**: Flat feet slow every reaction. You cannot move quickly, pivot, or generate power through the foot rotation.

**How to Fix**:
- Jump rope daily. This builds the calf endurance to stay on the balls of the feet.
- During shadow boxing, consciously lift the heels 1-2 cm off the floor and maintain that position.
- Do calf raises (3x20) after every training session.
- Set a timer to beep every 30 seconds during bag work. Each beep is a reminder to check foot position.

## 6. Telegraphing Punches

**What It Looks Like**: A visible windup or "tell" before a punch: pulling the hand back, dipping the shoulder, shifting weight obviously, or looking at the target before striking.

**Why It Happens**: The body naturally wants to load up for power by winding up, similar to throwing a ball.

**Why It Matters**: Any windup gives the opponent time to react. A telegraphed punch rarely lands against an experienced partner.

**How to Fix**:
- Throw from the guard position. No extra motion before the punch.
- Film yourself and watch for any pre-punch movement.
- Partner drill: have a partner call out every time they see a telegraph. This makes you aware of the habit.
- Understand that power comes from the hips and legs, not from a big arm windup.

## 7. Holding Breath

**What It Looks Like**: The fighter turns red, gasps for air between combinations, and fatigues much faster than they should.

**Why It Happens**: Concentration causes unconscious breath-holding. It is a stress response.

**Why It Matters**: Holding your breath causes early fatigue, increases blood pressure, and reduces reaction time.

**How to Fix**:
- Exhale sharply with every punch: a short "tss" or "shh" sound.
- The exhale serves double duty: it activates the core muscles for power and forces you to breathe.
- Practice breathing patterns during shadow boxing before adding the bag or partner.
- If you catch yourself holding your breath, stop, take three deep breaths, and resume at lower intensity.

## 8. Ignoring Defense

**What It Looks Like**: The fighter only practises offense. All bag work, all punching combinations, zero blocking, slipping, or footwork drills.

**Why It Happens**: Hitting things is more fun than practicing not getting hit. Offense provides immediate feedback (the bag moves); defense does not.

**Why It Matters**: A fighter who cannot defend will get overwhelmed in any realistic exchange. Defense is at least 50% of boxing.

**How to Fix**:
- Dedicate at least one-third of every training session to defense.
- Use the BoxBunny defense drill mode, which provides clear feedback on blocking and slipping success.
- Partner drills where one person only attacks and the other only defends. Switch roles each round.

## 9. Overcommitting to Single Punches

**What It Looks Like**: Throwing one big punch with maximum effort, then standing still and admiring the work.

**Why It Happens**: The instinct to end the fight with one punch. Movies and highlight reels reinforce this idea.

**Why It Matters**: Single punches are easy to avoid. Combinations are what create openings and overwhelm defenses. Standing still after a punch makes you a target.

**How to Fix**:
- Always throw in combinations of at least 2-3 punches.
- After every combination, move. Step to the side, step back, or pivot.
- "Punch and move" should become a reflex. Never admire your work.
- BoxBunny combo drills train the habit of multi-punch sequences.

## 10. Training Only Power, Ignoring Speed and Timing

**What It Looks Like**: Every punch is thrown at 100% force. No jabs, no feints, no light setup punches.

**Why It Happens**: Beginners equate effectiveness with power. The assumption is that harder = better.

**Why It Matters**: Speed beats power in boxing. A fast, accurate punch lands more often than a slow, powerful one. Timing (knowing when to throw) is more important than how hard you throw.

**How to Fix**:
- Spend at least one round per session throwing at 50% power with maximum speed.
- Practice feints: throw the motion of a punch without committing. This develops timing manipulation.
- Use the jab as a tool, not a weapon. The jab sets up everything else.
- BoxBunny reaction time drills specifically train speed over power.

## Summary

| Mistake | Core Fix |
|---------|----------|
| Dropping hands | Conscious return to guard after every punch |
| Closing eyes | Progressive partner exposure drills |
| No hip rotation | Isolate hip rotation before adding punches |
| Leaning forward | Step forward instead of reaching |
| Flat feet | Jump rope daily for calf endurance |
| Telegraphing | Throw from guard, no windup |
| Holding breath | Exhale sharply with every punch |
| Ignoring defense | Dedicate 1/3 of session to defense |
| Overcommitting | Always throw combinations, then move |
| Power-only focus | Train speed and timing as separate skills |
